## BIODIVERSITY HALLS OF FAME

● The Top Observers will be identified: Top 3 in each jurisdiction; the Top 10 in each State; and the 50 in the ecoregion (this can be done for both students and adults). This will form the basis of brand new Biodiversity Halls of Fame (BHoF). The initial emphasis will creating them in high schools and colleges/universities. These will be modelled after existing Sports Halls of Fame. 

● Creating BHoFs would be a way of recognizing and encouraging outstanding young naturalists, and giving them recognition they have never had within the school system. The best way to begin organizing these students would be by creating school-based Biodiversity Clubs. Eventually, there might even develop a long overdue National Biodiversity Corps. Enough publicity might be created so that the most outstanding students could be rewarded with Name, Image, Likeness (NIL) compensation, and be named Natural Heritage Heroes!

● BHoF induction criteria are under review, but would be primarily based on the number of “Research-Grade” observations of unique wild species, ensuring data quality and rewarding meaningful contributions. This approach addresses challenges faced by previous student-focused bioblitzes, such as low-quality observations and difficulty distinguishing wild from captured and/or cultivated species. Another criteria could include a minimum number of different species (i.e., 100 unique Research Grade Observations, at least 5 of which must be different species in the following categories: Plants, Invertebrates, Amphibians, Reptiles, Fish, Birds, and Mammals). By incentivizing generalist observation strategies, students are encouraged to explore diverse taxa, ecoregions, and habitats, fostering broader ecological literacy. Students could be recognized at the school, district, state, ecoregional, national and/or international levels.

More broadly, the establishment of BHoFs could: 

1. foster the creation of a replicable framework to recognize students who are interested in conservation.

2. build community leadership opportunities through bioblitz organization.

3. expand knowledge of, and engagement with, ecoregional biodiversity.

4. deepen awareness of natural vs. political boundaries.

5. shift the focus from “Natural Resources” to “Natural Heritage” in land use planning.

6. dramatically expand the pool of knowledgeable volunteers capable of monitoring species populations, including those of Greatest Conservation Need and Greatest Information Need identified in State Wildlife Action Plans. 

7. catalyze new conservation efforts, such as the creation of: new Xerces Society Bee Cities and Bee Campuses (https://beecityusa.org/); and efforts to establish statewide baseline data on pollinators (i.e., Oregon Native Bee Society and Bee Atlas (https://extension.oregonstate.edu/bee-atlas), and the Montana Moth Project (https://www.montanamothproject.org/).
